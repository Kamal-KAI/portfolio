# Three Years In — What I Wish I Knew

I recently hit three years as a data engineer. I almost missed it entirely. It was a Thursday. There was a Jira ticket open.

But when I noticed the date, I sat back and thought: *what would I tell myself on day one?*

## 1. The code you're embarrassed by is proof you've grown

My first production pipeline is still running somewhere. It's held together with string, hope, and a cron job no one dares touch. Every time I saw it, I cringed.

Now I think of it differently: the embarrassment means I've learned enough to know better. That *is* the progress.

## 2. Read the full error message

This sounds obvious. It is obvious. I still spent an embarrassing amount of year one Googling the first line of a stack trace without reading the rest.

The answer is almost always in line three or four.

## 3. No one is as confident as they appear

I've sat in rooms with senior engineers who spoke in certainties. Later, I watched them Google the same things I was Googling. Confidence isn't always knowledge — sometimes it's just comfort with uncertainty.

Learn to say "I'll find out" without flinching.

## 4. Document as you go

I wrote zero documentation in year one. Some in year two. By year three, it was a habit — and it's saved me hours every month.

The best time to write a runbook is right after solving the problem. That's when you remember what was confusing and what actually fixed it.

## What I'm Still Working On

- Saying no more often — to scope creep, to the 11 PM Slack ping
- Writing more (this post is part of that)
- Building in public instead of just building

Three years in. A lot more to go.
